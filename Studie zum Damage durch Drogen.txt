Addressing mental health in the UK
Mental illness is the largest single source of disease
burden in the UK. The cost to the National Health
Service is £10·4 billion a year (equivalent to almost
11% of its annual budget). The current estimate is that
about 11 million people of working age experience
mental health problems and about 5·5 million have a
common mental disorder. During the 2008–09 period,
11·4 million working days were lost in the UK due to
work-related stress, depression, and anxiety.
Mental ill health is associated with a greater risk of
physical illness. In the UK, individuals with mental health
disorders smoke almost half of the tobacco consumed
and account for half of all smoking-related deaths.
Depression doubles the risk of developing coronary heart
disease, and individuals with schizophrenia and bipolar
disorder die 20 years earlier than the general population.
Most disturbing is the fact that children from the poorest
20% of UK households have a three-times greater risk of
suffering from mental ill health than children from the
richest 20% of households. And one out of six children
in the UK lives in a workless household, the highest
proportion of any country in Europe.
These facts are why we welcome the UK’s Royal College
of Psychiatrists position statement, No health without
public mental health: the case for action, published on
Oct 26. The College calls for mental health to be placed
at the centre of the UK’s soon to be released national
public-health strategy. The statement advocates new
policies on mental health issues—to target public mental
health interventions toward those at high risk (children
in care, the homeless, and the unemployed), prioritise
anti-smoking programmes in mental health patients,
endorse the importance of mental health issues in older
sufferers, and promote healthy workplaces.
Mental health must be a public health issue.
Complementary and synergistic investment in mental
and physical health is a path toward improving
the quality of life, life expectancy, social function-
ing, and economic productivity of the UK and its
citizens. ■ The Lancet

Trick or treat or UNICEF Canada
This Halloween, across Canada, UNICEF’s logo is haunting
millions of Cadbury’s products. UNICEF has long been
associated with Halloween: since 1955, Canadian children
have collected more than CA$100 million for UNICEF, and
Oct 31 is National UNICEF Day. In 2008, UNICEF Canada
entered a corporate partnership in which Cadbury pledged
$500 000 over 3 years to fund construction of 25 schools
in Malawi and Rwanda. Cadbury also agreed to provide
promotional space on 4 million packets of treats each year
to allow UNICEF to raise awareness about their Schools for
Africa programme and to increase public donations.
But in a country where more than 20% of adults
are clinically obese, encouraging products which are
undeniably unhealthy is irresponsible. Some packaging
suggests that “Just by purchasing this product you are
helping to give children in Africa a chance at a better
life”. Amir Attaran of the University of Ottawa comments
“For an organization such as UNICEF whose raison d’être
is to promote childhood health and nutrition, and to do
so in an evidence-based way, this cuts straight to the
organization’s credibility”.
Corporate partnerships are essential for charities, but
at what cost? $500 000 is a drop in the ocean in the
context of UNICEF’s worldwide income of approximately
$3320 million in 2009. According to UNICEF’s policies,
such use of the logo is permitted. In fact, UNICEF Canada
is keen to develop corporate partnerships, describing a
“unique business opportunity” because “No other aid
organization engenders greater trust. None has greater
impact.” Clearly, UNICEF needs to take a hard look at
how they use their brand and consider the long-term
implications of their actions. Worryingly, UNICEF is also
“prepared to consider alliances with corporate affiliates
of companies in the alcohol or tobacco industry” even if
only under strict limits. The stringency and specificity of
guidelines for entering into corporate partnerships must
be increased to ensure that future decisions are in the
best interests of all children. In the current environment
of economic hardship, such relations could become
increasingly common as charities fight to survive and
big companies that sell unhealthy products find it easier
to buy institutional goodwill. ■ The Lancet

Like death and taxes, malaria is always with us. Not
normally in developed countries, but if “us” means
humanity, its burden in terms of mortality, morbidity, and
depressed economic productivity seems part of the human
condition. Much can and should be done to minimise its
burden, but eradication will probably require not simply
more money, but what W H Auden long ago styled “a
change of heart”. Malaria is still a disease of poverty.
Elucidating a new causative agent of disease, or its
treatment or mechanism of action, can lead to a Nobel
Prize—typhus, yellow fever, hepatitis, peptic ulcer, HIV, and
cervical cancer are some examples. Malaria has had two
Prizes awarded and both were contentious, for different
reasons. The first of these, and only the second Nobel
Prize in Physiology or Medicine awarded, in 1902, went
to Ronald Ross (1857–1932). It was fully deserved, since
Ross had shown experimentally that the malaria parasite
is transmitted through the bite of the mosquito. This
brought malaria (mal aria, “bad air”) from the realm of
swampy conditions and bad drainage, into the age of causal
specificity. It promised scientific eradication: get rid of the
Anopheles mosquitoes, and you will get rid of malaria.
Ross clearly deserved his award. But did he deserve it
alone? The Italian school of malariologists, led by Giovanni
Battista Grassi (1854–1925) thought not, and said so
publicly. Ross’s experimental model was for bird malaria, but
almost simultaneously, Grassi and his colleagues implicated
the Anopheles mosquito in the transmission of human
malaria. Grassi felt cheated out of his and his countrymen’s
just international recognition, and he and Ross carried on
an increasingly acrimonious priority dispute that ended
only with Grassi’s death. Grassi was overlooked not because
his research was unimportant, but because of its timing in
the history of Nobel Prizes. The Nobel committees these
days often make joint awards, up to the maximum of three
specified by Nobel’s will. They rarely did so in the early years
of the award, when single prizes were the default mode.
The second malaria Nobel Prize raised a different set
of issues. It was one of only two given for psychiatry (the
other one was in 1949 to the Portuguese neurologist and
neurosurgeon Antonio Egas Moniz, for the development
of frontal leucotomy to treat psychiatric disorders). In 1927,
the Austrian psychiatrist Julius Wagner-Jauregg (1857–1940)
received the ultimate accolade for a new therapy for what
was still a common condition within psychiatric hospitals:
the malaria treatment of general paralysis of the insane.
Ross, Grassi, and Wagner-Jauregg each deliberately infected
patients with malaria. Their aims and contexts were different,
but each man played with fire. Ross was born in India into a
British military family, although, like many children of the
Empire, he was raised by relatives in England. He would have
preferred to become a poet and man of letters, but economic
necessity (and his father) steered him into medicine. An
undistinguished student, he barely passed his exams and, in
1881, got one of the less prestigious postings in the Indian
Medical Service. He only gradually acquired a spirit of enquiry,
and on his second furlough back to England, in 1894, he
put himself in the way of Patrick Manson (1844–1922), the
leading tropical medicine expert in London. Manson had
already shown that the parasitic disease filaria is spread by
mosquitoes, and Manson suspected that malaria might also
be so spread. Ross returned to India determined to prove
Manson’s hypothesis.
The two men were separated by 4000 miles and had to
cope with a postal service that took up to 6 weeks for a reply.
Between 1895 and 1899, when Ross returned to England,
they exchanged more than 200 letters, which provide a
remarkable record of the making of a Nobel Prize. Almost
from the beginning, deliberate exposure to malaria was

The patriotism of these two correspondents was ardent: the
Italians, French, and Germans were all investigating malaria
transmission, and Ross and Manson were anxious that the
spoils should go to Britain (although both were Scotsmen,
they invariably wrote “England”). Ross for his part was only
too happy to try to infect soldiers in the Indian Army with
malaria. He was careful to tell Manson that his subjects had
consented, and his human experiments eventually included
placing mosquitoes next to them. Ross performed several
experiments of this kind, but they were before he realised
that only certain species of mosquito transmit malaria, so
his results were inconclusive. He himself contracted malaria
once during the 5 years he was corresponding with Manson
from India. By the time he was able to observe the developing
Plasmodia in the guts and salivary glands of Anophelines, he
had been posted to Calcutta, where there was no malaria. His
experimental proof of the role of infected mosquitoes in the
transmission of malaria was thus conducted on birds, with
the bird malaria parasite. By late 1898, the experiments were
complete and he resigned from the Indian Medical Service
and returned to England in February, 1899.
Working in Rome and the highly malarious Roman
Campania, Grassi had no trouble finding human malaria.
From late 1898 to late 1899, he and his colleagues showed
conclusively that previously healthy people could be infected
with malaria by being subjected to the bite of an infected
mosquito. He went further: only a female mosquito of
the genus Anopheles. He described his subjects always as
“volunteers”, and doctors at the time were aware that malaria
was one of the few diseases for which there was an effective
treatment: quinine. As if also to join the party, in 1900
Patrick Manson infected his own son, Thurburn, with an
infected mosquito, just days before the latter’s final medical
examinations. Thurburn dutifully developed all the signs of
malaria, was dosed with quinine and passed his exams.
Giving people malaria was an important step in establishing
the aetiology of the disease. Within two decades, malaria
had also become a therapeutic modality. The idea of one
disease casting out or preventing another disease has a long,
if marginal place in the history of medicine. Gout was the
quintessential disorder that was supposed to protect against
other maladies. Take care of your gout and you won’t be
bothered by other afflictions, doctors were prone to inform
their wealthy patients. Acute fevers, too, were sometimes
observed to have a positive benefit on other diseases.
From the late 19th century, doctors in psychiatric asylums
began to report that some patients got better after they
had had a bout of pneumonia, erysipelas, typhoid, or other
febrile illness. They became more rational, less delusional,
and altogether better able to relate to their surroundings.
Wagner-Jauregg became intrigued with these publications,
and from the late 1880s, began giving his patients injections
of various substances, to see if they would improve. He tried
tuberculin, the spirilla of recurrent fever, the streptococcus
of erysipelas, and finally, the Plasmodium of malaria, given as
a blood transfusion from an acutely ill donor. He tried these
treatments on a variety of his psychiatric patients, but his
careful record keeping and rough case controlling suggested
that patients with general paralysis of the insane were most
likely to benefit from his therapies. That general paralysis of
the insane was a consequence of end-stage syphilis had been
shown by August von Wassermann (of the Wassermann
test) in 1906, and confirmed by Noguchi in 1912. Was this
a possible causal mechanism: the organism was simply killed
by the heat of the fever?
Wagner-Jauregg’s decision that a malarial infection was the
best remedy was reached only after a good deal of trial and
error. His patients were dysfunctional and institutionalised,
and thereby in no position to refuse. He combined his
malaria therapy with the accepted treatment for syphilis,
mercury, iodine, and neo-salvarsan. The combination of all
four worked better than the ordinary anti-syphilis therapies
by themselves. A course of quinine therapy followed the
acute fever, and his reported results indicated that more than
half of his patients benefited to such an extent that they
could be released from the hospital, and in some instances,
resume their former occupations.
Wagner-Jauregg proselytised his new treatment in papers
and conferences before and after World War I, and it was
taken up in many countries, with some success, despite
frequent reports that “cures” were temporary and relapses
common. In Britain, the Horton Mental Hospital, a psychiatric
hospital run by the London County Council in Surrey, became
the fulcrum for the therapy. The laboratory there was
run by Sydney Price James (1870–1946), a distinguished
malariologist, and he and his colleagues supplied the infected
mosquitoes (instead of blood transfusions) and used the
human experiments to study clinical manifestations of
malaria, its periodicity, and the various strains of the parasite
that can affect human beings. They also used the treatment
to compare some newer anti-malarial drugs with quinine.
Careful as his record keeping was, Wagner-Jauregg’s seems
hardly the stuff of Nobel Prizes. It was, as he admitted, only
an empirical investigation. We should not judge the past
by standards of the present, however, and general paralysis
of the insane was a common, debilitating, and ultimately
fatal disorder. That his therapy was rendered obsolete by
penicillin should not allow us retrospectively to denigrate the
Nobel Committee’s decision. Dangerous diseases may need
dangerous remedies.

Addressing mental health in the UK
Mental illness is the largest single source of disease
burden in the UK. The cost to the National Health
Service is £10·4 billion a year (equivalent to almost
11% of its annual budget). The current estimate is that
about 11 million people of working age experience
mental health problems and about 5·5 million have a
common mental disorder. During the 2008–09 period,
11·4 million working days were lost in the UK due to
work-related stress, depression, and anxiety.
Mental ill health is associated with a greater risk of
physical illness. In the UK, individuals with mental health
disorders smoke almost half of the tobacco consumed
and account for half of all smoking-related deaths.
Depression doubles the risk of developing coronary heart
disease, and individuals with schizophrenia and bipolar
disorder die 20 years earlier than the general population.
Most disturbing is the fact that children from the poorest
20% of UK households have a three-times greater risk of
suffering from mental ill health than children from the
richest 20% of households. And one out of six children
in the UK lives in a workless household, the highest
proportion of any country in Europe.
These facts are why we welcome the UK’s Royal College
of Psychiatrists position statement, No health without
public mental health: the case for action, published on
Oct 26. The College calls for mental health to be placed
at the centre of the UK’s soon to be released national
public-health strategy. The statement advocates new
policies on mental health issues—to target public mental
health interventions toward those at high risk (children
in care, the homeless, and the unemployed), prioritise
anti-smoking programmes in mental health patients,
endorse the importance of mental health issues in older
sufferers, and promote healthy workplaces.
Mental health must be a public health issue.
Complementary and synergistic investment in mental
and physical health is a path toward improving
the quality of life, life expectancy, social function-
ing, and economic productivity of the UK and its
citizens. ■ The Lancet

Trick or treat or UNICEF Canada
This Halloween, across Canada, UNICEF’s logo is haunting
millions of Cadbury’s products. UNICEF has long been
associated with Halloween: since 1955, Canadian children
have collected more than CA$100 million for UNICEF, and
Oct 31 is National UNICEF Day. In 2008, UNICEF Canada
entered a corporate partnership in which Cadbury pledged
$500 000 over 3 years to fund construction of 25 schools
in Malawi and Rwanda. Cadbury also agreed to provide
promotional space on 4 million packets of treats each year
to allow UNICEF to raise awareness about their Schools for
Africa programme and to increase public donations.
But in a country where more than 20% of adults
are clinically obese, encouraging products which are
undeniably unhealthy is irresponsible. Some packaging
suggests that “Just by purchasing this product you are
helping to give children in Africa a chance at a better
life”. Amir Attaran of the University of Ottawa comments
“For an organization such as UNICEF whose raison d’être
is to promote childhood health and nutrition, and to do
so in an evidence-based way, this cuts straight to the
organization’s credibility”.
Corporate partnerships are essential for charities, but
at what cost? $500 000 is a drop in the ocean in the
context of UNICEF’s worldwide income of approximately
$3320 million in 2009. According to UNICEF’s policies,
such use of the logo is permitted. In fact, UNICEF Canada
is keen to develop corporate partnerships, describing a
“unique business opportunity” because “No other aid
organization engenders greater trust. None has greater
impact.” Clearly, UNICEF needs to take a hard look at
how they use their brand and consider the long-term
implications of their actions. Worryingly, UNICEF is also
“prepared to consider alliances with corporate affiliates
of companies in the alcohol or tobacco industry” even if
only under strict limits. The stringency and specificity of
guidelines for entering into corporate partnerships must
be increased to ensure that future decisions are in the
best interests of all children. In the current environment
of economic hardship, such relations could become
increasingly common as charities fight to survive and
big companies that sell unhealthy products find it easier
to buy institutional goodwill. ■ The Lancet

provides early acceptance to the
medical school for about 35 college
sophomores every year who agree
to major in a humanities subject
in return for not having to take
organic chemistry, physics, or the
Medical College Admission Test.
Students in the programme this
year have worked as professional
actors, lived with shaman healers
in Peru, taught English in Indonesia
as Fulbright scholars, and pursued
advanced degrees in classical music
performance.
As undergraduates, we were given
the freedom to follow our passions
outside of medicine, armed with
the reassurance of medical school
acceptance. One study 2 found that
students in the Mount Sinai pro-
gramme compared favourably with
their classmates in nearly all cat-
egories measured, including Med-
ical School Performance Evaluation
final performance ranking, honours
grades in clerkships, and Compass II
scores.
We are the lucky students who
were never forced to forego a class
in health policy because it conflicted
with organic chemistry or made to
spend our summers in a laboratory
rather than study European politics
and economics in the UK. I hope that
more medical schools heed Barr’s
advice and afford their students
the same freedom of study that I
have had; it will enable providers to
marshal the creativity and wisdom
of many disciplines necessary to heal
patients and our broken health-care
system.

provides early acceptance to the
medical school for about 35 college
sophomores every year who agree
to major in a humanities subject
in return for not having to take
organic chemistry, physics, or the
Medical College Admission Test.
Students in the programme this
year have worked as professional
actors, lived with shaman healers
in Peru, taught English in Indonesia
as Fulbright scholars, and pursued
advanced degrees in classical music
performance.
As undergraduates, we were given
the freedom to follow our passions
outside of medicine, armed with
the reassurance of medical school
acceptance. One study 2 found that
students in the Mount Sinai pro-
gramme compared favourably with
their classmates in nearly all cat-
egories measured, including Med-
ical School Performance Evaluation
final performance ranking, honours
grades in clerkships, and Compass II
scores.
We are the lucky students who
were never forced to forego a class

spend our summers in a laboratory

more medical schools heed Barr’s
advice and afford their students
the same freedom of study that I

marshal the creativity and wisdom

patients and our broken health-care
system.

Like death and taxes, malaria is always with us. Not
normally in developed countries, but if “us” means
humanity, its burden in terms of mortality, morbidity, and
depressed economic productivity seems part of the human
condition. Much can and should be done to minimise its
burden, but eradication will probably require not simply
more money, but what W H Auden long ago styled “a
change of heart”. Malaria is still a disease of poverty.
Elucidating a new causative agent of disease, or its
treatment or mechanism of action, can lead to a Nobel
Prize—typhus, yellow fever, hepatitis, peptic ulcer, HIV, and
cervical cancer are some examples. Malaria has had two
Prizes awarded and both were contentious, for different
reasons. The first of these, and only the second Nobel
Prize in Physiology or Medicine awarded, in 1902, went
to Ronald Ross (1857–1932). It was fully deserved, since
Ross had shown experimentally that the malaria parasite
is transmitted through the bite of the mosquito. This
brought malaria (mal aria, “bad air”) from the realm of
swampy conditions and bad drainage, into the age of causal
specificity. It promised scientific eradication: get rid of the
Anopheles mosquitoes, and you will get rid of malaria.
Ross clearly deserved his award. But did he deserve it
alone? The Italian school of malariologists, led by Giovanni
Battista Grassi (1854–1925) thought not, and said so
publicly. Ross’s experimental model was for bird malaria, but
almost simultaneously, Grassi and his colleagues implicated
the Anopheles mosquito in the transmission of human
malaria. Grassi felt cheated out of his and his countrymen’s
just international recognition, and he and Ross carried on
an increasingly acrimonious priority dispute that ended
only with Grassi’s death. Grassi was overlooked not because
his research was unimportant, but because of its timing in
the history of Nobel Prizes. The Nobel committees these
days often make joint awards, up to the maximum of three
specified by Nobel’s will. They rarely did so in the early years
of the award, when single prizes were the default mode.
The second malaria Nobel Prize raised a different set
of issues. It was one of only two given for psychiatry (the
other one was in 1949 to the Portuguese neurologist and
neurosurgeon Antonio Egas Moniz, for the development
of frontal leucotomy to treat psychiatric disorders). In 1927,
the Austrian psychiatrist Julius Wagner-Jauregg (1857–1940)
received the ultimate accolade for a new therapy for what
was still a common condition within psychiatric hospitals:
the malaria treatment of general paralysis of the insane.
Ross, Grassi, and Wagner-Jauregg each deliberately infected
patients with malaria. Their aims and contexts were different,
but each man played with fire. Ross was born in India into a
British military family, although, like many children of the
Empire, he was raised by relatives in England. He would have
preferred to become a poet and man of letters, but economic
necessity (and his father) steered him into medicine. An
undistinguished student, he barely passed his exams and, in
1881, got one of the less prestigious postings in the Indian
Medical Service. He only gradually acquired a spirit of enquiry,
and on his second furlough back to England, in 1894, he
put himself in the way of Patrick Manson (1844–1922), the
leading tropical medicine expert in London. Manson had
already shown that the parasitic disease filaria is spread by
mosquitoes, and Manson suspected that malaria might also
be so spread. Ross returned to India determined to prove
Manson’s hypothesis.
The two men were separated by 4000 miles and had to
cope with a postal service that took up to 6 weeks for a reply.
Between 1895 and 1899, when Ross returned to England,
they exchanged more than 200 letters, which provide a
remarkable record of the making of a Nobel Prize. Almost
from the beginning, deliberate exposure to malaria was

est pro patria mori. But don’t do it yourself”.
The patriotism of these two correspondents was ardent: the
Italians, French, and Germans were all investigating malaria
transmission, and Ross and Manson were anxious that the
spoils should go to Britain (although both were Scotsmen,
they invariably wrote “England”). Ross for his part was only
too happy to try to infect soldiers in the Indian Army with
malaria. He was careful to tell Manson that his subjects had
consented, and his human experiments eventually included
placing mosquitoes next to them. Ross performed several
experiments of this kind, but they were before he realised
that only certain species of mosquito transmit malaria, so
his results were inconclusive. He himself contracted malaria
once during the 5 years he was corresponding with Manson
from India. By the time he was able to observe the developing
Plasmodia in the guts and salivary glands of Anophelines, he
had been posted to Calcutta, where there was no malaria. His
experimental proof of the role of infected mosquitoes in the
transmission of malaria was thus conducted on birds, with
the bird malaria parasite. By late 1898, the experiments were
complete and he resigned from the Indian Medical Service
and returned to England in February, 1899.
Working in Rome and the highly malarious Roman
Campania, Grassi had no trouble finding human malaria.
From late 1898 to late 1899, he and his colleagues showed
conclusively that previously healthy people could be infected
with malaria by being subjected to the bite of an infected
mosquito. He went further: only a female mosquito of
the genus Anopheles. He described his subjects always as
“volunteers”, and doctors at the time were aware that malaria
was one of the few diseases for which there was an effective
treatment: quinine. As if also to join the party, in 1900
Patrick Manson infected his own son, Thurburn, with an
infected mosquito, just days before the latter’s final medical
examinations. Thurburn dutifully developed all the signs of
malaria, was dosed with quinine and passed his exams.
Giving people malaria was an important step in establishing
the aetiology of the disease. Within two decades, malaria
had also become a therapeutic modality. The idea of one
disease casting out or preventing another disease has a long,
if marginal place in the history of medicine. Gout was the
quintessential disorder that was supposed to protect against
other maladies. Take care of your gout and you won’t be
bothered by other afflictions, doctors were prone to inform
their wealthy patients. Acute fevers, too, were sometimes
observed to have a positive benefit on other diseases.
From the late 19th century, doctors in psychiatric asylums
began to report that some patients got better after they
had had a bout of pneumonia, erysipelas, typhoid, or other
febrile illness. They became more rational, less delusional,
and altogether better able to relate to their surroundings.
Wagner-Jauregg became intrigued with these publications,
and from the late 1880s, began giving his patients injections
of various substances, to see if they would improve. He tried
tuberculin, the spirilla of recurrent fever, the streptococcus
of erysipelas, and finally, the Plasmodium of malaria, given as
a blood transfusion from an acutely ill donor. He tried these
treatments on a variety of his psychiatric patients, but his
careful record keeping and rough case controlling suggested
that patients with general paralysis of the insane were most
likely to benefit from his therapies. That general paralysis of
the insane was a consequence of end-stage syphilis had been
shown by August von Wassermann (of the Wassermann
test) in 1906, and confirmed by Noguchi in 1912. Was this
a possible causal mechanism: the organism was simply killed
by the heat of the fever?
Wagner-Jauregg’s decision that a malarial infection was the
best remedy was reached only after a good deal of trial and
error. His patients were dysfunctional and institutionalised,
and thereby in no position to refuse. He combined his
malaria therapy with the accepted treatment for syphilis,
mercury, iodine, and neo-salvarsan. The combination of all
four worked better than the ordinary anti-syphilis therapies
by themselves. A course of quinine therapy followed the
acute fever, and his reported results indicated that more than
half of his patients benefited to such an extent that they
could be released from the hospital, and in some instances,
resume their former occupations.
Wagner-Jauregg proselytised his new treatment in papers
and conferences before and after World War I, and it was
taken up in many countries, with some success, despite
frequent reports that “cures” were temporary and relapses
common. In Britain, the Horton Mental Hospital, a psychiatric
hospital run by the London County Council in Surrey, became
the fulcrum for the therapy. The laboratory there was
run by Sydney Price James (1870–1946), a distinguished
malariologist, and he and his colleagues supplied the infected
mosquitoes (instead of blood transfusions) and used the
human experiments to study clinical manifestations of
malaria, its periodicity, and the various strains of the parasite
that can affect human beings. They also used the treatment
to compare some newer anti-malarial drugs with quinine.
Careful as his record keeping was, Wagner-Jauregg’s seems
hardly the stuff of Nobel Prizes. It was, as he admitted, only
an empirical investigation. We should not judge the past
by standards of the present, however, and general paralysis
of the insane was a common, debilitating, and ultimately
fatal disorder. That his therapy was rendered obsolete by
penicillin should not allow us retrospectively to denigrate the
Nobel Committee’s decision. Dangerous diseases may need
dangerous remedies.

Grasping the nettle of mental illness in prisons
The UK Government has pledged to reduce prison numbers by diverting mentally ill individuals
away from the criminal justice system and into the health-care system. Talha Burki reports.
In May, 2010, the number of prisoners
in England and Wales stood at 84 982,
an increase of 60% since 1995.
Nowhere in western Europe jails more
of its citizens, nor a higher proportion
of its population.
Last month, Chancellor George
Osborne announced the measures by
which the UK Government aims to cut
public spending by some £80 billion
over the next 4 years. The Ministry of
Justice has had its budget trimmed
by about a quarter, in light of which
it pledged to reduce prison numbers
by 3000 by 2014. Part of this will
be delivered by diverting mentally
disordered individuals away from the
criminal justice system into the health-
care system, predominantly in the
community, although some cases will
require a secure ward. In fact, this is a
policy supported by UK Governments
dating back to the 1990s, but its
implementation has been inconsistent
and lacklustre. This looks likely
to change. There is a cross-party
consensus on the issue, commitment
from the upper echelons of the
Cabinet, and a forthcoming Health
and Criminal Justice Programme,
central to which is diversion.

Healing the scars of torture
The Programme for Survivors of Torture at New York’s Bellevue Hospital is a beacon of hope for the
thousands who flee to the city to escape persecution elsewhere in the world. Sharmila Devi reports.
In a modern room at New York’s
Bellevue Hospital, Marie’s story of rape
and torture by African soldiers sounded
like a medieval chronicle, but it was an
all-too familiar tale for Adam Keller, the
doctor who examined her.
Although Keller has heard countless
similar stories in his 15 years of heading
the Programme for Survivors of Torture
(PSOT), he listened to Marie’s story
with such empathy and understanding
that it was as though he was hearing
everything for the first time.
“Five people came to get me. I
asked if I could change my clothes but
they said no and put me in the car”,
said Marie, before exhaling a deep
sigh. “Then prison and everything
happened. They broke my nose and cut
me on the leg with a knife. It was a very
small room and I couldn’t sleep. I was
2 weeks in prison. I was in a cell all by
myself. It was very dirty.” “I know this is
hard to tell but were you raped, sexually
assaulted?” asked Keller. Marie nodded.
She was raped and sodomised.
Marie, who is 36 years old and did
not want to reveal her real name or
any details that might identify her,
suffered her ordeal in an African
country in May, 2006. Last September,
she found her way to New York, where
she knows few people and has lived
in a succession of homeless shelters
while awaiting asylum status. A few
months ago, she was admitted with
suspected malaria to Bellevue, which is
affiliated with the New York University
School of Medicine, and is the oldest
public hospital in the USA to treat the
underserved. Marie tested negative
for HIV but was treated for syphilis
and hepatitis.
She was also referred to the PSOT,
where a psychiatrist prescribed sleeping
pills and started therapy for profound
post-traumatic stress disorder. Keller’s
medical examination in late September
was part of the process of documenting
her wounds, both physical and mental,
that would provide evidence for her
asylum application. “It’s my privilege
to be your medical doctor”, Keller told
Marie as she silently wept. “We can’t
erase the past but we will do everything
we can to help you.”
The PSOT provides a full range
of medical, psychiatric, and social
services, and has seen practically
every method of torture that persists
around the world—gang rape, beating,
amputation of limbs and hands. But
compared with physical symptoms,
doctors say that it is rebuilding trust
and healing the mental wounds
inflicted through torture that is
hardest and takes the longest.
The programme’s 15 full-time
and 40 part-time health and social
professionals receive about five
referrals a week, and there is a waiting
list of about 2 months. It has treated
about 3000 individuals from more
than 80 countries, and treated about
700 people last year alone. Estimates
of the number of torture survivors in
the USA are as high as 200 000, with
perhaps about 70 000 in New York

immigrant networks.
The PSOT draws on the wide range of
expertise found within the Bellevue and
New York University public–academic
partnership, including plastic surgeons
for reconstructive surgery, physical
therapists, and specialists in infectious
disease. Many of the programme’s staff
have experience of working abroad,

camps on the Cambodia–Thailand

has enabled him to not only run the

When an illness is retired from the
Diagnostic and Statistical Manual
of Mental Disorders (DSM)—as will
happen to dementia when DSM-V
is published in 2013—it reminds
us of the inescapable human role
in shaping disease categories and
attendant social meanings.
As Perminder Sachdev suggests, the
disappearance of dementia should
allow for more humane treatment
of ageing people in our societies who
genuinely suffer along with their
carers. For centuries, “dementia” has
been freighted with meaning that
denotes a particular “type” of person.
To be “demented” evokes images of
extreme disability that go beyond
mere cognitive impairment, and has
serious social, legal, and economic
consequences for those so-labelled
who must live intimately with the
meanings of their illness.
The proposed replacement
for dementia is “neurocognitive
disorders of the major and minor
type”. 1 If adopted, how might
this terminology affect social
meanings? Perhaps the emphasis
on “disorder” can help foster an
understanding of brain ageing as
occurring on a continuum rather
than being organised into discrete
disease categories partitioning
“the normal” from “the demented”
(although one worries about the
value-laden major/minor qualifiers).
On the other hand, if society does
not change the way it values ageing
people, any new terminology used
to describe cognitive deterioration is
likely to acquire the same pejorative
connotations as dementia.
Maurits van den Noort and Peggy
Bosch agree that “more respect for…
ageing is needed and that different
medical terminology might be a
powerful linguistic instrument to
achieve this”, but voice concern that
we are going too soft. Choosing
“weak” language to describe the
phenomenology of brain ageing risks
lowering the priority status of the
condition among those who control
public coffers.
I sympathise with the realpolitik
faced by policy advocates, but
question the wisdom of constantly
framing the challenges of brain
ageing as a dramatic race to obtain
funding for care and support services,
treatment, and cure research.
Shouldn’t a civilised society be able to
summon public support for a health
challenge that will affect us all and
cost hundreds of billions of dollars 2
without using severe language that
foments fear, deepens stigma, and
obviates meaningful contribution?
As dementia enters its twilight, I
would prefer to see a new language
of solidarity emerge that evokes
empathy for people more severely
affected rather than just fear and
sadness, while reminding us of
our shared vulnerability to ageing
processes and the essential unity this
creates across the generations.

Simon D’Alton for their input. I declare that I have

In April, 2010, the β ketoamfetamine
stimulant mephedrone (4-methyl-
methcathinone) and several similar
compounds were classified as
Class B substances in the UK under
the Misuse of Drugs Act. Legislation
was prompted by public health
concerns, with the expectation that
these controls would limit availability
and use. 1
As part of our group’s ongoing
research into new synthetic drugs, we
did an online survey of 150 mephe-
drone users (average age 24 years)
in June, 2010. We have compared our
findings with those from a previous
online survey we did in November,
2009, 2 and a telephone interview of
users in 2010 before the legislation. 3
Our key objective in this comparison
was to assess whether the new
legislative control of mephedrone
had affected its availability and use.
Of the 150 respondents to the 2010
survey, 95 (63%) reported that they
had continued to use mephedrone
since the law had changed. 52 of
these respondents (55%) said that
they intended to continue using the
same amount of mephedrone, and 38
(40%) reported that they would now
use less. 85 respondents (57%) had
bought mephedrone from a dealer,
an increase of almost 40% from the
41% who had reported purchasing
from a dealer in the telephone study
of users done before legislation. In
the current 2010 survey, the mean
price per gram paid for mephedrone
was £16 (mode, £20), compared
with a mean price of £10 when the
drug was obtainable online before
legislation.

classification of mephedrone has
had a limited effect on controlling
its availability and use. Before the
introduction of the legislation, users
generally obtained mephedrone via
the internet. Now they buy it from
street dealers, on average at double
the price. We suspect that, in time,
there are likely to be reductions in
purity, and increases in health harms.

The twilight of
dementia
Daniel George (Aug 21, p 586)1 is
correct about the importance of
semantic choice in adapting to the
challenge posed by ageing popu-
lations. His dissatisfaction with the
term “dementia” is obvious. Its use
by the medical profession arouses
great anxiety in patients, evoking
images of extreme disability and
dependence. The diagnosis has
serious consequences in terms of a
person’s competence in employment,
legal transactions, driving, and even
the conduct of ordinary activities
of living, far beyond what might be
immediately suggested by the level of
cognitive impairment.
The term has many limitations, the
most important of which is the lack of
an operationalised definition. There
is no consensus on what constitutes
“impairment” in a particular cognitive
domain or “significant impairment
in social or cognitive functioning”,
thereby compromising the reliability
of the diagnosis between raters
and across sets of criteria. 2 The con-
sequences of the term conspire
against an early diagnosis, which is of
concern as the promise of preventive
strategies comes closer. The equation
of dementia with Alzheimer’s disease
in the public mind does a disservice
to the multiple causes of cognitive
impairment, with only one in two
cases of dementia being due to
Alzheimer’s disease.
The time has come to slowly retire
this term, 3 with the recognition that
cognitive dysfunction is on a con-
tinuum, and that it is important to
recognise it early and identify its cause,
with no need for an individual to bear
the cross of a dementia diagnosis.

Nicholas Cohen
Epidemiologist committed to global health who
worked in deprived communities in Africa and
Asia. Born on April 9, 1938, in Cape Town, South
Africa, he died on Sept 15, 2010, in Esher, UK.
It was in 1968 that Nicholas Cohen, then aged 30 years,
answered a three line job advertisement in The Lancet. A
35-bed mission hospital at Mantsonyane in central Lesotho
needed a doctor. Cohen applied, and got the job. With a
first in medicine from Cambridge University and experience
gained at Guy’s Hospital in London and the American
Hospital in Paris he was embarking on a career quite unlike
anything he would have experienced in Europe. For much of
the rest of his life he worked predominantly with the poorest
of the poor in Africa and Asia. And when not working among
them he would still be championing their cause.
The son of a successful surgeon in private practice in
London, Cohen clearly knew what he was giving up. But
although he could hardly have known exactly what he was
getting into when he set off for Lesotho, he relished his
new life. In his own words he “learned what it means to live
on one meal of maize flour a day, what it feels like to go
barefoot, clothed only in a blanket in the freezing winter
days, and how many children die of diseases that elsewhere
are entirely preventable.” Returning to the UK, in 1971, he
studied epidemiology at the London School of Hygiene and
Topical Medicine and then worked in the Department of
Community Health at Nottingham University. But the pull
of far-distant places was not to be denied and 1976 saw
him travelling again, this time as part of the WHO’s smallpox
eradication campaign in Ethiopia. “We tracked smallpox”, he
wrote, “from the green fertile slopes of Arussi, across the sand
scarred start of the great African Rift valley at Awash, along
the vertiginous eastern escarpment of Shoa and north west
with the Afar towards the Danakil desert.”
His next move was to Bangladesh where he lived for more
than a decade, working first for the Save the Children Fund.
It was in Dacca that he met Anthony Measham, now of the
World Bank but then employed by the Ford Foundation
which was supporting projects on children’s nutrition. Cohen
was working as a clinician as well as running epidemiological
and other studies around the country. Measham recalls how
Cohen befriended one particular young beggar in Dacca who
was suffering from tuberculosis, and then devoted much
time and effort to an ultimately unsuccessful attempt to save
him. But why the attention to one among so many? “Because
empathy and compassion were not, for him, abstract ideals
but feelings that had to be played out in helping individuals”,
says Measham. Richard Semba, professor of ophthalmology
at Johns Hopkins University School of Medicine, also met
Cohen for the first time in Bangladesh—in his case through
their mutual interest in vitamin A. Cohen carried out a survey
of the risk factors for vitamin deficiency and nutritional
blindness in children. But even when doing epidemiology—
focused, by definition, on the big picture—he still had time
for the individual. “This is what made him a physician who
had a really good understanding of vitamin deficiency”, says
Semba. “He knew it on the personal level. It wasn’t just some
sort of academic exercise.” In 1988, Cohen moved back to
Europe to work for WHO on immunisation. Not a natural
bureaucrat he nevertheless battled to achieve support for
his ideas. By the mid-1990s he had begun another and more
personal struggle against the progressive limitations on his
life imposed by the symptoms of Parkinson’s disease.
Cohen’s son Yvan describes his father as always keen
to take the path less trodden. “The predictable route of
becoming a doctor and working in the UK just wasn’t
exciting or challenging enough for him. He was somebody
who devoured life, who was curious about almost
everything, who loved people and had a strong thirst for
adventure. Most of all I think he wanted to give his life
meaning. This was why he chose the path of public health
and to work in the developing world where he hoped he
might also make some difference to other people’s lives.”
Measham endorses that assessment. “He was very left
wing, very focused on community action rather than top
down government”, he says. And Semba agrees: “A kind
man, partly driven I think by the idealism of the sixties. He
liked to connect with ordinary people. There was always
something about him that was very approachable.” Cohen
is survived by a wife, Nancy, two sons, and two daughters.

Richard Feachem—scaling the heights of global health leadership
Richard Feachem’s first foray into development came soon
after he left school as a VSO volunteer. His mission—to build
an airfield on the south coast of Guadalcanal in the Solomon
Islands. “I swam ashore through heavy breakers to the village
of Haimarau. A year later, an airstrip was completed and
functions to this day. This experience entirely changed my
life and gave me commitment, as well as some early insight
and understanding, for a career devoted to development.”
As Executive Director of Global Health Sciences at the
University of California San Francisco (UCSF), development
remains at the heart of Feachem’s work and his broad
vision is informed by a varied career. After a degree in
civil engineering, a PhD in environmental health, a Doctor
of Science degree in Medicine, and completing the Top
Management Programme in the UK Cabinet Office, Feachem
went on to hold public health positions at the World Bank,
the London School of Hygiene and Tropical Medicine
(LSHTM), and WHO. In 2002, he was both surprised and
delighted to be offered the founding Executive Directorship
of the Global Fund to Fight AIDS, Tuberculosis and Malaria.“It
proved to be a once-in-a-life-time opportunity to craft from
scratch an entirely new instrument for development finance”,
he says. Feachem is proud of what the Fund has achieved, but
is disappointed by the recent replenishment. “This partially
reflects the impact of the global financial crisis, an impact
which will get worse before it gets better. It also reflects the
need for the Global Fund to return to first principles; namely,
to be a lean, demand-driven, arms-length, non-prescriptive,
social venture capitalist. There has been drift away from
this original vision. A return to first principles is essential to
guarantee the next decade of the Global Fund’s existence.”
Feachem’s views on the Fund reflect his wider
engagement in the politics of global health. According to

The day of diabetes: coming soon to a person near you
World Diabetes Day happens every year on Nov 14. It
marks the birthday of Frederick Banting, who, along
with Charles Best, had the idea that the pancreas’
internal secretion, which regulates blood sugar,
might hold the key to the treatment of diabetes.
They were right, and insulin was discovered in 1922.
World Diabetes Day was launched in 1991 by the
International Diabetes Federation (IDF) and WHO
in response to concerns about the escalating health
threat from diabetes. Each year a new theme is chosen
by the IDF to address issues facing the global diabetes
community. The campaign slogan for 2010 is “Let’s
take control of diabetes. Now”.
A recent report from the UK’s Quality and Outcomes
Framework (QOF) Exception Data 2009/10 includes
information on the incidence and prevalence of type 2
diabetes and obesity. Figures were collected through
general practices and show that the population with
obesity has risen by more than 265 000 to 5·5 million
in the UK over the past year. Also, the number of
people diagnosed with type 2 diabetes in the UK
increased by more than 150 000 to 2·8 million in
2009–10. One in 20 of the population is being treated
for diabetes, and one in ten for obesity. Around 10%
of National Health Service spending goes on diabetes
and its complications—equivalent to £9 billion per
year or £1 million per hour. “The obesity-fuelled type 2
diabetes epidemic is a clear example of where the new
Coalition Government’s rhetoric of tackling health
problems through prevention must be turned into
action”, says Simon O’Neil, Diabetes UK’s director of
care, information, and advocacy. Guidance suggests
that people must keep up their consumption of five
portions of fruit and vegetables a day, engage in daily
physical activity, and be provided with prominent
and regular warnings of the potentially devastating
consequences of an unhealthy lifestyle.
The situation in the USA is much worse. In 2007,
the combined costs of types 1 and 2 diabetes
were over US$174 billion. The National Diabetes
Surveillance System of the Centers for Disease
Control and Prevention (CDC) has estimated that
the future prevalence of diagnosed diabetes could be
substantially worse than that previously suggested. A
large increase in diabetes prevalence could be driven
by multiple factors, including increased incidence,
better detection, and greater incidence and prevalence
among immigrant populations. The prevalence of total
diabetes (diagnosed and undiagnosed) in the USA is
forecast to increase from its current level of about one
in ten adults to between one in five and one in three
adults by 2050. The health-care costs of a person with
diagnosed diabetes are about 2·3 times higher than
a person without diabetes. Additionally, the CDC’s
model makes the brave assumption that the mortality
rate of the diabetic population will decline at least as
much as that of the non-diabetic population as health-
care outcomes improve over time (ie, the mortality
rate ratio associated with diabetes will be constant).
This estimate might well be too optimistic.
The data published in the UK and USA did not
consider people aged under 18 years. But a report
by Australia’s Productivity Commission found that
many schemes to prevent childhood obesity, which
is a risk factor for type 2 diabetes, failed to make a
substantial impact among young people in Australia.
The latest figures show that the rate of obesity in
boys aged up to 17 years has risen dramatically in the
past two decades to 10%, with a further 16% deemed
overweight. The rise in prevalence of obesity in girls
has levelled off at about 5%, with about 18% deemed
overweight. Obesity cost the Australian Government
Aus$58·2 billion in 2008, according to Diabetes
Australia, which comprised Aus$8·3 billion of financial
costs and Aus$49·9 billion in lost wellbeing—due to
disability or shorter life span. The report concluded
that the most cost-effective interventions for obesity
prevention were a 10% tax on junk food, restricting
unhealthy food advertising to children, and the
labelling of food packaging with a traffic-light system.
As the health risks of obesity and diabetes multiply
across developing countries, deaths from diabetes
are likely to increase by more than 50% over the
next 10 years without urgent action. Governments
everywhere must act now to tackle this global public
health issue. Preventive public health policies need
to be designed and implemented based on cost-
effectiveness, with a focus on gathering evidence,
information sharing, evaluation, and continuing
policy refinement. ■ The Lancet

Author’s reply
When an illness is retired from the
Diagnostic and Statistical Manual
of Mental Disorders (DSM)—as will
happen to dementia when DSM-V
is published in 2013—it reminds
us of the inescapable human role
in shaping disease categories and
attendant social meanings.
As Perminder Sachdev suggests, the
disappearance of dementia should
allow for more humane treatment
of ageing people in our societies who
genuinely suffer along with their
carers. For centuries, “dementia” has
been freighted with meaning that
denotes a particular “type” of person.
To be “demented” evokes images of
extreme disability that go beyond
mere cognitive impairment, and has
serious social, legal, and economic
consequences for those so-labelled
who must live intimately with the
meanings of their illness.
The proposed replacement
for dementia is “neurocognitive
disorders of the major and minor
type”. 1 If adopted, how might
this terminology affect social
meanings? Perhaps the emphasis
on “disorder” can help foster an
understanding of brain ageing as
occurring on a continuum rather
than being organised into discrete
disease categories partitioning
“the normal” from “the demented”
(although one worries about the
value-laden major/minor qualifiers).
On the other hand, if society does
not change the way it values ageing
people, any new terminology used
to describe cognitive deterioration is
likely to acquire the same pejorative
connotations as dementia.
Maurits van den Noort and Peggy
Bosch agree that “more respect for…
ageing is needed and that different
medical terminology might be a
powerful linguistic instrument to
achieve this”, but voice concern that
we are going too soft. Choosing
“weak” language to describe the
phenomenology of brain ageing risks
lowering the priority status of the
condition among those who control
public coffers.
I sympathise with the realpolitik
faced by policy advocates, but
question the wisdom of constantly
framing the challenges of brain
ageing as a dramatic race to obtain
funding for care and support services,
treatment, and cure research.
Shouldn’t a civilised society be able to
summon public support for a health
challenge that will affect us all and
cost hundreds of billions of dollars 2
without using severe language that
foments fear, deepens stigma, and
obviates meaningful contribution?
As dementia enters its twilight, I
would prefer to see a new language
of solidarity emerge that evokes
empathy for people more severely
affected rather than just fear and
sadness, while reminding us of
our shared vulnerability to ageing
processes and the essential unity this
creates across the generations.

Simon D’Alton for their input. I declare that I have
no conflicts of interest.

findings with those from a previous
online survey we did in November,

users in 2010 before the legislation. 3

legislative control of mephedrone
had affected its availability and use.

41% who had reported purchasing

Daniel George (Aug 21, p 586)1 is
correct about the importance of
semantic choice in adapting to the
challenge posed by ageing popu-
lations. His dissatisfaction with the
term “dementia” is obvious. Its use
by the medical profession arouses
great anxiety in patients, evoking
images of extreme disability and
dependence. The diagnosis has
serious consequences in terms of a
person’s competence in employment,
legal transactions, driving, and even
the conduct of ordinary activities
of living, far beyond what might be
immediately suggested by the level of
cognitive impairment.
The term has many limitations, the
most important of which is the lack of
an operationalised definition. There
is no consensus on what constitutes
“impairment” in a particular cognitive
domain or “significant impairment
in social or cognitive functioning”,
thereby compromising the reliability
of the diagnosis between raters
and across sets of criteria. 2 The con-
sequences of the term conspire
against an early diagnosis, which is of
concern as the promise of preventive
strategies comes closer. The equation
of dementia with Alzheimer’s disease
in the public mind does a disservice
to the multiple causes of cognitive
impairment, with only one in two
cases of dementia being due to
Alzheimer’s disease.
The time has come to slowly retire
this term, 3 with the recognition that
cognitive dysfunction is on a con-
tinuum, and that it is important to
recognise it early and identify its cause,
with no need for an individual to bear
the cross of a dementia diagnosis.

Author’s reply
When an illness is retired from the
Diagnostic and Statistical Manual
of Mental Disorders (DSM)—as will
happen to dementia when DSM-V
is published in 2013—it reminds
us of the inescapable human role
in shaping disease categories and
attendant social meanings.
As Perminder Sachdev suggests, the
disappearance of dementia should
allow for more humane treatment
of ageing people in our societies who
genuinely suffer along with their
carers. For centuries, “dementia” has
been freighted with meaning that
denotes a particular “type” of person.
To be “demented” evokes images of
extreme disability that go beyond
mere cognitive impairment, and has
serious social, legal, and economic
consequences for those so-labelled
who must live intimately with the
meanings of their illness.
The proposed replacement
for dementia is “neurocognitive
disorders of the major and minor
type”. 1 If adopted, how might
this terminology affect social
meanings? Perhaps the emphasis
on “disorder” can help foster an
understanding of brain ageing as
occurring on a continuum rather
than being organised into discrete
disease categories partitioning
“the normal” from “the demented”
(although one worries about the
value-laden major/minor qualifiers).
On the other hand, if society does
not change the way it values ageing
people, any new terminology used
to describe cognitive deterioration is
likely to acquire the same pejorative
connotations as dementia.
Maurits van den Noort and Peggy
Bosch agree that “more respect for…
ageing is needed and that different
medical terminology might be a
powerful linguistic instrument to
achieve this”, but voice concern that
we are going too soft. Choosing
“weak” language to describe the
phenomenology of brain ageing risks
lowering the priority status of the
condition among those who control
public coffers.
I sympathise with the realpolitik
faced by policy advocates, but
question the wisdom of constantly
framing the challenges of brain
ageing as a dramatic race to obtain
funding for care and support services,
treatment, and cure research.
Shouldn’t a civilised society be able to
summon public support for a health
challenge that will affect us all and
cost hundreds of billions of dollars 2
without using severe language that
foments fear, deepens stigma, and
obviates meaningful contribution?
As dementia enters its twilight, I
would prefer to see a new language
of solidarity emerge that evokes
empathy for people more severely
affected rather than just fear and
sadness, while reminding us of
our shared vulnerability to ageing
processes and the essential unity this
creates across the generations.

Simon D’Alton for their input. I declare that I have
no conflicts of interest.

Tracking the fever trail of malaria
In a recent “training of trainers” malaria
course for doctors in a malaria-endemic
country, I arranged for delegates
to visit local hospitals to examine
what actually happened to patients
with malaria. What they discovered
was frequent use of artemisinin
monotherapy, underdosing with
quinine, and protracted parenteral
therapy in hospital for those who could
easily have taken treatment by mouth
at home. Several patients were said
to have parasitaemia despite 8 days
of artemether therapy, a situation
which suggested erroneous dosing,
substandard drugs, faulty microscopy,
or artemisinin-resistant parasites.
Any of these possibilities should
be a cause for alarm and inquiry,
but nobody seemed worried. That
faulty microscopy was a contributing
problem became evident when an
expert rechecked 120 malaria “positive”
films and found that 115 contained no
detectable parasites.
One of many powerful messages
of Sonia Shah’s The Fever is that what
is actually happening on the ground
is commonly different from what is
meant, or believed, to be going on.
Malaria-control activities are well based
on evidence acquired by research, but
their effectiveness ultimately depends
on what people actually do. And this
has implications for monitoring:
“Equating distribution (of impregnated
nets) with use is like counting the bars
of soap in a hospital ward rather than
the number of clinicians with clean
hands”, notes Shah.
Shah’s book is a wide-ranging
examination of the history of malaria
and what we have tried to do about it.
She writes with the touch of a novelist.
Her main protagonists—parasites
and mosquitoes as well as people—
are endowed with character and
motivation. Resistant parasites and
mosquitoes are “grizzled survivors” of
long campaigns against them, malaria
makes a “malevolent homecoming” to
an area recently free of it, plasmodia
“teem with purpose” inside the
veins of house sparrows outside her
window. The plot is enjoyable, in part
because of the hindsight we can bring
to many of the scenes. Slaves carried
with them from Africa parasites that
could then be spread in the southern
colonies of North America and would
cause much more disease in their
new owners than in themselves, a
phenomenon noted and puzzled over
by contemporary doctors who knew
nothing of plasmodia or immunity.
This prompts a vague unease in the
reader—how many of our current
dilemmas will one day cause amused
sympathy among historians, who
will know answers that right now lie,
unrecognised, under our noses?
The overall impact of The Fever is
sobering. Many high hopes, many
successes, many boastful predictions,
and an equal array of dashed
expectations, unreached goals, and
resurgences of disease. This overview
is surely salutary; it reminds us to stop
underestimating the challenge, stop
believing in single-fix solutions, and
recognise the need for a long, slow,
unrelenting, and concerted grind
towards a hoped-for victory. One lesson
that we have taken a long time to learn
relates to the dangers of success. The
obvious efficacy of a new intervention—
Shah gives such examples as chloro-
quine, dichlorodiphenyltrichloroethane
(DDT), and bednets—seems commonly
to deflect attention away from any
potential limitations, and from the fact
that any one approach is unlikely to be
the final solution, may contribute to its
own failure, and will be more difficult
to deliver than to conceive. In this
regard Shah compares us as a species

does not re-enact failed strategies and
weak defences, its historical memory
shot”. Success has often been, and
may be again, one of our greatest
challenges—how will we motivate
people, governments, and funders
to care enough about malaria to
keep it at bay as we make advances
towards elimination and the potential
destructiveness of the disease becomes
less obvious?

be locally owned and directed, if they
are to have a chance of a long-term
impact. This is particularly true of
malaria control, depending as it does
on a complex web of infrastructure
that extends from roads, land use,
and housing to people’s beliefs and
domestic behaviour. Shah notes how
many millions of dollars have been
directed to providing control materials
without imparting the capability to
develop, produce, or use these locally.
Local ownership requires a long-term
view alongside efforts to promote
capacity. There are many current
moves in the right direction, including
training fellowships and capacity-

But there are still many more malaria
experts outside endemic countries
than within them.

come out of this account with flying

commonly held more sway than data
and logic. In World War II, troops

Brigadier Fairley—”the most eminent
malariologist in the world”—said
the drug was still efficacious, when

Addressing mental health in the UK
Mental illness is the largest single source of disease
burden in the UK. The cost to the National Health
Service is £10·4 billion a year (equivalent to almost
11% of its annual budget). The current estimate is that
about 11 million people of working age experience
mental health problems and about 5·5 million have a
common mental disorder. During the 2008–09 period,
11·4 million working days were lost in the UK due to
work-related stress, depression, and anxiety.
Mental ill health is associated with a greater risk of
physical illness. In the UK, individuals with mental health
disorders smoke almost half of the tobacco consumed
and account for half of all smoking-related deaths.
Depression doubles the risk of developing coronary heart
disease, and individuals with schizophrenia and bipolar
disorder die 20 years earlier than the general population.
Most disturbing is the fact that children from the poorest
20% of UK households have a three-times greater risk of
suffering from mental ill health than children from the
richest 20% of households. And one out of six children
in the UK lives in a workless household, the highest
proportion of any country in Europe.
These facts are why we welcome the UK’s Royal College
of Psychiatrists position statement, No health without
public mental health: the case for action, published on
Oct 26. The College calls for mental health to be placed
at the centre of the UK’s soon to be released national
public-health strategy. The statement advocates new
policies on mental health issues—to target public mental
health interventions toward those at high risk (children
in care, the homeless, and the unemployed), prioritise
anti-smoking programmes in mental health patients,
endorse the importance of mental health issues in older
sufferers, and promote healthy workplaces.
Mental health must be a public health issue.
Complementary and synergistic investment in mental
and physical health is a path toward improving
the quality of life, life expectancy, social function-
ing, and economic productivity of the UK and its
citizens. ■ The Lancet

Trick or treat or UNICEF Canada
This Halloween, across Canada, UNICEF’s logo is haunting
millions of Cadbury’s products. UNICEF has long been
associated with Halloween: since 1955, Canadian children
have collected more than CA$100 million for UNICEF, and
Oct 31 is National UNICEF Day. In 2008, UNICEF Canada
entered a corporate partnership in which Cadbury pledged
$500 000 over 3 years to fund construction of 25 schools
in Malawi and Rwanda. Cadbury also agreed to provide
promotional space on 4 million packets of treats each year
to allow UNICEF to raise awareness about their Schools for
Africa programme and to increase public donations.
But in a country where more than 20% of adults
are clinically obese, encouraging products which are
undeniably unhealthy is irresponsible. Some packaging
suggests that “Just by purchasing this product you are
helping to give children in Africa a chance at a better
life”. Amir Attaran of the University of Ottawa comments
“For an organization such as UNICEF whose raison d’être
is to promote childhood health and nutrition, and to do
so in an evidence-based way, this cuts straight to the
organization’s credibility”.
Corporate partnerships are essential for charities, but
at what cost? $500 000 is a drop in the ocean in the
context of UNICEF’s worldwide income of approximately
$3320 million in 2009. According to UNICEF’s policies,
such use of the logo is permitted. In fact, UNICEF Canada
is keen to develop corporate partnerships, describing a
“unique business opportunity” because “No other aid
organization engenders greater trust. None has greater
impact.” Clearly, UNICEF needs to take a hard look at
how they use their brand and consider the long-term
implications of their actions. Worryingly, UNICEF is also
“prepared to consider alliances with corporate affiliates
of companies in the alcohol or tobacco industry” even if
only under strict limits. The stringency and specificity of
guidelines for entering into corporate partnerships must
be increased to ensure that future decisions are in the
best interests of all children. In the current environment
of economic hardship, such relations could become
increasingly common as charities fight to survive and
big companies that sell unhealthy products find it easier
to buy institutional goodwill. ■ The Lancet
